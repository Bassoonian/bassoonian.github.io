§lang_0's reconstructed consonant inventory as seen above can be considered relatively standard. The allophone status of [ŋ] was presumably already established back in Proto-Indo-European while it is likely that §lang_0 also featured a [ɱ] for its labiodental additions. [z] was originally an allophone in Proto-Indo-European, where it came into being through voicing assimilation, but received a phonemic status in early Carite thanks to Engels' Law and Verner's Law (see below).

It is worth noting that this inventory is reconstructed and the exact details are not entirely known. For example, a significant amount of caritologists believe that the labiodental fricatives were in fact regular labial in their execution, which would also make the reconstruction of an allophonic labiodental nasal redundant. They base their evidence on the labial nature of their origin (that is, the labiovelars they originated from) as well as scanty evidence that surrounding languages featured labial fricatives rather than labiodental ones. However, others have suggested [β] was an allophone of /w/ word-initially preceding a liquid (which would explain its oddity in terms of phonotactics), considering such a sound was likely the intermediate step between [w] and [b], its likely reflex in very early §lang_1. As *vl- was preserved unaltered, some argue this means that the fricatives were in fact labiodental in §lang_0. Another common albeit controversial allophone posited is [ʐ] for /z/ after voiced obstruents, citing the legality of onset clusters with /z/ as second element such as /vz/ and /gz/, whose unvoiced equivalents are illegal.

§lang_0's reconstructed vowel inventory mostly reflects the Late Proto-Indo-European vowel inventory with its typical five-vowel /a e i o u/ and short-long distinction; however, it features two noteworthy additions: the vowels /yː øː/. [ø] was an allophone of its long equivalents when shortened by Osthoff's Law. It is worth noting that /y/, albeit a marginal phoneme, was in all likelihood phonemic. While not a native sound, it appeared in loans from Greek. Some have recently begun questioning the true monophthong status of /øː/, reconstructing a single diphthong /oi̯/ instead. However, this is a baseless assumption.

Phonotactics

||PHONOTACTICS||

Suprasegmentals

Stress fell on the first syllable of the stem - as such, prefixes such as the augment were never stressed.

Morphophonology

Besides an active regressive voicing assimilation filter (see below), we may find the collapse of two identical vowels into a single long vowel of the same quality, i.e. |V₁V₁(ː)| /V₁ː/.
