Syntax

A significant portion of §lang_0 syntax is reconstructed. The few attestations we have access to do not provide us with more than basic constructions. As such, a great portion of this chapter has been reconstructed through comparison with contemporary languages in its vicinity, as well as the syntax of later Carite.

Verb Phrase

The verb is the central element of any clause and, without exception, must be present in all clauses. A verb phrase is the combination of a verb and its objects, of which the number and type differ according to the type of verb, predicate complements and additional adverbials.

Objects

As mentioned before, the number and type of objects depend on the type of the verb. In the case of an intransitive verb, the object is excluded, while in the case of a transitive verb, it may be required.

Person and Number

As previously seen in the chapter on morphology, §lang_0 verbs inflected for person and number, reflecting three persons, first, second and third, and two numbers, singular and plural. Indicative

The indicative objectively described an event or state of affairs, particularly in main clauses. It utilised °DONAWT° as its negative marker.

  • ||SYN-17||

Subjunctive

The subjunctive almost exclusively appeared in subordinate clauses. It likewise used °DONAWT° to mark negations.

  • ||SYN-18||

Imperative

The imperative was far more limited in terms of tense, person and number. Three forms exist, namely the second person singular and the first and second persons plural. The second person forms were used for commands or prohibitions, while the first person plural form can be roughly equated with "let us". The negative particle, also referred to as prohibition particle in this case, of the imperative was °REALLYDONAWT°.

Voice

Proto-Indo-European possessed a synthetic distinction between active and mediopassive, which, as previously described in the chapter on verbal morphology, split into two separate verbal paradigms. In turn, we find a new analytical way to represent the passive voice, which contrasts with the simplex active endings. This passive was formed by inflecting the copula alongside the verb's past participle.
