Nominal Morphology

Developments

Modern loss of cases

While the attestations in a more archaic register of §lang_4 cleanly continue to reflect the four-case system of §lang_3 - that is, nominative, accusative, genitive and dative - we find a significantly different usage in the modern register, more specifically regarding the usage of the nominative and accusative cases. In nouns, the nominative case appears to have been usurped by the accusative case entirely, while in adjectives, it the nominative case is only used adverbially. In personal pronouns, however, the clear distinction between nominative and accusative appears to remain. Since §lang_4 is generally analysed from an archaic point of view - that is, one with a nominative/accusative distinction - it is important to be aware of the vastly different usage of these cases in modern inscriptions. However, with the generalisation of the accusative forms in all future phases of Carite evolution, the dictionary form that is employed from §lang_4 onwards tends to be the accusative for nouns and adjectives instead.

Modern loss of neuter

With the generalisation of the accusative forms in the modern register, the only difference between masculine and neuter forms would be the accusative plural forms. We can, however, safely say that the masculine forms were completely generalised in the modern register, with them only remaining distinct in the archaic register. This notion is confirmed by the presence of overarchaicisms - the usage of a neuter plural form for an etymologically masculine noun.

Modern replacement of genitive plural

In the modern register, the primary genitive plural was outright replaced by the primary dative plural, not unlike the merger found in the conjunctives. The original genitive plural form remained in use in the archaic register.

Stress shifts

Due to the loss of previously word-final vowels, stress had phonemicised by §lang_4 times, although it was under significant restrictions. In various cases, however, the stress is not where we would have regularly expected it, presumably due to analogy with other forms. In the primary forms, all non-nominative final "ghost syllables" were eliminated - more specifically, genitive singular and dative (and modern genitive) plural - resulting in regular stress for all primary forms. This contrasted with the the conjunctive forms, which often had their stress one syllable later. In the genitive and dative plural forms in the conjunctives, the final ghost syllable was likewise deleted, yielding the same stress as the other conjunctive forms.

Loss of Exon

With the loss of many word-final vowels, we already found a gradual merger of Exon's nouns into the regular inflection back in §lang_3. This process must have extended and completed by §lang_4 times as we no longer find any Exon's nouns in neither register.

Degrees of comparison

With both comparatives and superlatives inducing i-umlaut, thematic adjectives occasionally showed stem alteration if in another degree than the positive. This alteration affected the stem regardless of gender, leading to some adjectives having three distinct stems (and some not undergoing any alteration at all). Iotic adjectives, on the other hand, never showed this alteration due to already having been affected by i-umlaut in their positive stem.

Comparative

Due to its unique inflection pattern, the comparative degree does not distinguish between adjectival and substantival usage. The comparative mostly continues its usual pattern, with the exception of the nominative plural, which appears to have been minorly reformed through analogy with the other forms. The original palatal element of the suffix has fully integrated into the stem, palatalising the last consonant per the usual palatalisation rules.
