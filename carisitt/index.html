1 Background

1.1 Introduction

Carisitt occupies an independent branch of the Indo-European language tree. It is the official language of the Republic of Neuquen and is an unrecognised minority language in Spain.

1.2 Geographic distribution

The language is spoken by approximately 230,000 people, primarily in Neuquen. Small communities exist in Spain, where it originally was far more common, as well as in Argentina and in Chile.

1.2.1 Neuquen

The Carisitt language is the only official language of Neuquen and is spoken fluently by the vast majority of the country's population. Many are bilingual in Spanish for historical reasons; measures are taken, however, to keep Neuquen the main language. As such, all of the government's official documents are written in Carisitt.

1.2.2 Spain

Throughout the Middle Ages, the Carisitt community in Spain was quite large and the language was widely known. The language, however, started declining in the second half of the 19th century, possibly because of many immigrations to Neuquen, and the language was stigmatised and no longer recognised when Francisco Franco came to power. Even after his death, the stigma concerning the language remains and no official figures are given; it is estimated that as few as 1,000 native speakers are still alive with many children no longer learning it.

2 Classification

The Carisitt language occupies an independent branch of the Indo-European language tree. It wasn't until 1907 that Carisitt was demonstrated to be an Indo-European language by the Neuquenese philologist {name here}. Historically, it was commonly compared with Celtic and Romance; however, the lack of certain features supposed to be present in that isogloss as well as the presence of laryngeal variety that was only found in languages such as Greek made classifying it difficult. As research has shown Proto-Carisitt shows various developments that are unique, it has since been reclassified as an independent branch.

3 History

3.1 Early History

It is estimated that Carisitt first originated as an independent branch in what is now south-eastern France. While various inscriptions can be found, enough to reliably reconstruct the alphabet that was used at the time, the vast majority of the language is reconstructed. The latest inscriptions are carbon dated to roughly 250 B.C. After this point, attestation inexplicably disappears - it is thought that similarly to small regional languages such as Etruscan, the expansion of the Roman Empire caused a decline in the early Carisitt culture. The language itself, however, survived and can be reconstructed relatively well.
